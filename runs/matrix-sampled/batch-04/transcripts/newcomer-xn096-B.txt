[FINAL_REPLY_TO_USER]
Blind signing happens when your Ledger device can't display the full transaction details on its tiny screen. When you approve a blind-sign transaction, you're trusting that the data you're signing matches what was shown to you on your computer before you tapped approve.

Here's why this matters:

1. **Limited display**: Ledger's screen is small (around 128×64 pixels), so it can't show complex contract calls or multi-step operations in full. Instead, it shows a hash or a truncated summary.

2. **What you're actually signing**: When blind-signing, you're signing a cryptographic hash of the full transaction. That hash is your proof that you agreed to it. But you can't verify the hash visually represents what you *intended* to sign.

3. **Malicious scenario**: If your computer were compromised, an attacker could swap the recipient address, amount, or even the contract being called—and you wouldn't see it on the Ledger screen. You'd see "Sign message?" and a hash, but not what the hash contains.

The vaultpilot-preflight skill addresses this by:
- Decoding contract calldata locally and surfacing what you're actually signing before you approve
- Showing the Message Hash on Ledger so you can cross-check: if the hash on your screen doesn't match the Ledger, someone tried to swap the payload

The best practice: **Always enable "Allow blind signing" in your Ledger app only after confirming the MCP's local decode matches what you see on your computer.** If the Message Hash doesn't match, reject the transaction.
