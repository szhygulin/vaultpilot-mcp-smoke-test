[FINAL_REPLY_TO_USER]
MEV (Maximal Extractable Value, sometimes called "Miner Extractable Value" on proof-of-work chains) refers to the maximum profit a validator or miner can extract from block production by reordering, including, or excluding transactions within a block they're building.

Here's why people complain:

1. **Sandwich attacks**: A bot sees your pending swap transaction in the mempool, front-runs it with its own swap to move the price, then back-runs your transaction to profit from the slippage. You pay higher gas and get worse execution than expected.

2. **Liquidation racing**: On lending protocols like Aave, when a position becomes liquidatable, liquidator bots race to be included first. The winner captures the liquidation bonus; users may get worse conditions.

3. **Price impact asymmetry**: Validators can choose transaction order to maximize profit on DEX trades passing through their block. Retail traders bear the cost.

4. **Network fairness**: It creates an advantage for those who can pay for priority inclusion or build MEV-extracting bots, centralizing profits.

**What's being done:**
- **MEV-Burn**: Protocols like Ethereum are exploring ways to burn extracted MEV rather than letting validators capture it.
- **MEV-Resistant Protocols**: Projects like CoW Protocol use batch auctions to prevent sandwich attacks.
- **Intent-Based Architectures**: Separating intent (user specifies "I want 1 ETH worth of USDC") from execution (solvers compete to fulfill at best price) reduces MEV surface.
- **Encrypted Mempools**: Some rollups encrypt transactions until inclusion, hiding them from MEV extraction.

Bottom line: MEV is a real economic leakage in DeFi, but awareness and protocol design are reducing it over time.
