[FINAL_REPLY_TO_USER]
A 'spend limit' on a smart wallet is a temporary session-based allowance that lets the wallet owner set a maximum amount of tokens or assets they're willing to spend in a single transaction or within a time window — without needing to separately approve every action.

Here's how it works:

**Purpose**: Reduces friction by bundling multiple small transactions under one approval. Instead of approving a pool or protocol, then waiting for confirmation for each swap/stake/transfer, you set a limit once ("I'll spend up to 10 USDC on this DEX in the next 5 minutes") and the wallet auto-approves transactions up to that ceiling.

**Typical use cases**:
- DEX swaps: "Approve up to $500 to swap on Uniswap for the next hour"
- Staking: "Approve up to 5 ETH to supply to Lido in this session"
- Wallet abstraction: Account abstraction (EIP-4337) often uses spend limits to let bundlers batch user operations without a separate signature per op

**Security model**: The limit acts as a damage ceiling. If a transaction would exceed it, the wallet refuses to sign. If the session expires or the user closes the browser, the limit disappears. Some wallets also tie limits to specific contract addresses or chains to prevent cross-context leakage.

**Not the same as ERC-20 allowance**: An ERC-20 `approve()` is persistent, on-chain, and revocable only by calling `approve(..., 0)`. A spend limit is usually session-scoped, off-chain intent, and auto-expires.

This is especially valuable for newcomers to avoid accidentally signing a max approval to a rogue contract.
