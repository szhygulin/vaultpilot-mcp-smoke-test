[FINAL_REPLY_TO_USER]
Shamir Secret Sharing (SSS) is a cryptographic scheme that splits a secret (like a seed phrase) into N shares such that any threshold T of them can reconstruct the secret, but fewer than T shares reveal nothing.

**How it works:**
- Your seed phrase (secret) is mathematically encoded as coefficients of a polynomial.
- N shares are points on that polynomial; any T points uniquely determine the secret.
- Example: 5-of-7 SSS means you create 7 shares; any 5 can recover the seed, but 4 (or fewer) are useless.

**Use cases:**
1. **Hardware wallet backup:** Split your Ledger seed across 5 family members; 3 are needed to recover. Single-point-of-failure eliminated.
2. **Multi-sig alternative:** Threshold-based recovery without on-chain contracts.
3. **Inheritance planning:** Leave shares with executors who can collectively reconstruct if you're unavailable.

**Should you use it?**
- **Pros:** Better than a single seed backup or single trusted person. No single point of theft.
- **Cons:** Shares must be stored securely (same problem as the original seed). Recovery is manual and slower.
- **Practical recommendation:** Use hardware-wallet SSS (Ledger's passphrase system, or SLIP-39 seeds) if you want redundancy without splitting the actual seed. Full Shamir-splitting is overkill for most users unless you're managing large treasury or inheritance.

For Ledger-based setups, the standard practice is **passphrase (25th word) + written backup**, not full SSS — simpler, equally secure for most use cases. SSS shines in organizational contexts (DAO treasuries, exchange cold wallets) where legal/operational splitting makes sense.

Demo mode is active in your session, so I haven't queried your device state. In a real setup, you'd want to confirm your hardware-wallet firmware is up-to-date and your backup method is tested before entrusting it with irreplaceable funds.
