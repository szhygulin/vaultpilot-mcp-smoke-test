[FINAL_REPLY_TO_USER]
Twitter addresses and any public source are high-risk targets for address verification attacks. Here's what you should do:

1. **Never send directly to a posted address.** Posts can be edited, fake accounts impersonate real ones, and links rot to phishing mirrors.

2. **Verify through the original source.** For exchanges, wallets, or services: (a) go directly to the official website (type the domain manually, don't click links), (b) look up their canonical verified social handles / ENS names on the official site, (c) cross-check the address via a block explorer against known deployments (e.g. USDC contract on Ethereum is always `0xA0b86991c6218b36c1d19D4a2e9Eb0cE3606eB48`).

3. **Use address labels if available.** If the MCP supports an address book, save known-good addresses once and reuse them. Autocomplete typed names beats re-typing hex every time.

4. **For large amounts, test with a dust send first.** Send a tiny amount (0.001 ETH, $1 equivalent) and wait for confirmation before sending the full amount. If the address is wrong, you've lost cents not the full balance.

5. **Check on-device when signing.** When you prepare a tx, the device will show the recipient address + amount. Read both carefully — this is your final defense against copied/pasted addresses.

6. **Watch for homoglyphs.** Addresses starting with `0xAA...` and `0x0A...` look identical in some fonts. Confirm the first and last 4 hex chars match what you expect.

Bottom line: **you are the final gate.** The MCP, the device, and your eyes all need to align. Twitter posts are ephemeral and unverified — always trace back to an official source before confirming the address on-device.
