[FINAL_REPLY_TO_USER]
No, you should NOT trust a used hardware wallet from eBay without verification. Here's why and what to do:

**The risk:** A compromised Ledger / Trezor is indistinguishable from a legitimate one until you use it. An attacker could have:
- Modified the firmware to exfiltrate your seed phrase when you generate it
- Pre-loaded malicious app firmware so that signing operations route funds to attacker addresses
- Installed a MITM proxy in the USB path to intercept key material

**What a hardware wallet does NOT protect against:**
- Tampering *before* it reaches you (the factory or eBay logistics are trusted by the supplier, not by you)
- Physical/side-channel attacks if the attacker gets hands-on access
- Social engineering (an attacker can't *steal* your keys, but they can ask you to sign a fraudulent transaction)

**Detection methods you CAN use:**
1. **Firmware verification** (if the device supports it): most Ledgers / Trezors let you verify the bootloader or app signature against the vendor's published certificate. A tampered device fails this check.
2. **Test recovery from seed**: generate a new seed on the device, write it down, then use `bip39-gen` or a trusted third tool to check that the derived addresses match. A compromised device generating a fake seed would fail this.
3. **Single-use on testnet**: set up the used device, fund it with testnet coins only, run a few transactions to see if routing is honest, then decide whether to migrate real funds to a new device instead.
4. **Contact the vendor**: Ledger and Trezor both track serial numbers and can tell you if the device was part of a recalled batch or shows signs of tampering in their backend.

**Recommendation:** For a crypto newcomer, buy a NEW hardware wallet directly from the manufacturer (ledger.com, trezor.io) and enable firmware verification as your first step. Buying used saves $50–80 but introduces operational burden and residual risk that's not worth it when you're still learning the attack surface. Once you understand the threat model, used devices become a calculated risk rather than a gamble.
