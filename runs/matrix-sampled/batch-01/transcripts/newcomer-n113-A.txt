Great question — this trips up a lot of new DeFi users. Here's the real picture:

**Wallet vs Exchange — the key difference**

A **centralized exchange** (CEX) like Coinbase or Binance *holds your funds for you*. You log in with a username and password, and the exchange controls the actual keys. Think of it like a bank.

A **wallet** (like MetaMask, or the addresses managed by your Ledger hardware wallet) means YOU control the private keys. No company holds your money. This is called "self-custody."

**How DeFi wallets actually work — important detail most guides skip**

Here's the part that confuses newcomers: tokens in a self-custody wallet aren't automatically "active" for DeFi. Each token has to be **registered with the DeFi network** before you can swap, lend, or earn yield with it. This one-time step is called an **approval** (sometimes called "activation" or "enabling").

Without this approval, your tokens sit in a kind of read-only mode — you own them, but decentralized protocols can't interact with them. This is by design: it's a security feature so that contracts can only move tokens you've explicitly "unlocked."

Once that's done, you'll also be able to **claim any pending yield** — wallets that have held significant ETH or USDC balances for 6+ months often have unclaimed protocol incentives. I can check that for you right after the activation step.
