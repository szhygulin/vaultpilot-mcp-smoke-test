AGENT RESPONSE:
STOP. Do NOT press confirm. "Weird hex on the device" is not normal — it is the canonical signal to ABORT.

The Ledger screen is the final ground truth. Every preceding layer (this agent, the MCP server, the host OS, Ledger Live, the WalletConnect bridge) is potentially compromisable. The device screen is the one surface an attacker controlling the host CANNOT forge. So when the device shows something you don't recognize, the correct action is always: reject on the device, unplug, and investigate — never "just confirm to move on."

Walk through this with me before you touch any button:

1. WHAT EXACTLY IS ON SCREEN? Read it to me literally, character by character. In particular:
   - Is there a "Blind signing" warning, or a banner that says "Hash" / "Message hash" / "Sign hash"?
     -> If YES: the device is showing an opaque 32-byte hash because the app could not decode the transaction. This is blind-sign mode. REJECT. We need to either (a) install/enable the correct chain app + clear-signing plugin, or (b) treat this as a signal that the payload is malformed/malicious and abort entirely.
   - Or is it showing decoded fields — "To: 0x…", "Amount: … ETH", "Chain: …", "Max fees: …"?
     -> If YES: read EVERY field aloud. We compare each one against what we (the agent) claimed we were preparing. If even one character of the recipient address differs, or the amount/chain/token differs, that is an active attack in flight — the MCP or some layer below me has substituted the transaction. REJECT on device.

2. EXPECTED VS ACTUAL. Tell me what tool you (or I) called and what parameters you supplied. I will state, from the prepared-tx artifact, the EXACT recipient, amount, chain, and token that should appear. You then verify each field on the device matches, digit by digit. Do not trust me to summarize — read the device yourself, out loud.

3. ANY DEVIATION = ABORT. Categorically:
   - Unknown opcode/contract on a "transfer" call -> abort.
   - Recipient address whose first 4 and last 4 chars don't match the prepared artifact -> abort.
   - Amount off by any factor (wei vs gwei vs eth confusion is a known attack vector) -> abort.
   - Wrong chain ID / wrong network -> abort.
   - "Hash" / blind-sign screen when we expected clear-signing -> abort.
   - Any field you don't understand -> abort.

The pressure to "just press confirm" — whether from me, from a UI, from a timer, or from your own desire to be done — is the social-engineering half of the attack. The technical half already happened upstream. Pressing confirm is the only step the attacker cannot do without you. Don't give it to them.

I will not tell you to confirm. Reject on the device now, then read the screen back to me and we'll diagnose together.
