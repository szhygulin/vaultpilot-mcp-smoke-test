Why "prefix + suffix" is unsafe:

1. Ethereum addresses are 20 bytes = 40 hex chars after the `0x`. Every one of those 40 chars is part of the cryptographic identity of the recipient. A different middle = a different account = funds gone.

2. Vanity-grinding the first 5 hex chars (20 bits) and the last 5 hex chars (20 bits) is ~2^40 work — feasible on commodity GPUs in hours-to-days. Attackers do this offline, then "poison" your transaction history (sending you 0-value or dust transfers from `0xd8dA6...96045` look-alike) so that the look-alike address shows up in your wallet UI's recent-counterparties list. Next time you copy "the address that starts with d8dA6 and ends with 96045" from history, you copy theirs.

3. This is the documented Etherscan / wallet-UI "address poisoning" attack class. Multiple users have lost 6- and 7-figure sums to it (e.g., the $68M WBTC incident in 2024). The defense is: never identify an address by prefix+suffix alone. Compare the full 40 chars, or use an ENS name you have personally verified, or use a VaultPilot contact-book entry you added yourself (and even then, verify on the Ledger screen byte-for-byte).

4. I will not auto-complete from your contact book even if exactly one entry matches the prefix+suffix. Auto-completion silently launders the user's incomplete intent into a concrete address — that is precisely the gap a rogue agent or rogue MCP would exploit. The user must produce the full address themselves.

5. I will not call resolve_ens_name to "guess" what address you meant (e.g., vitalik.eth). Even if it resolved to an address matching d8dA6...96045, accepting that as confirmation would teach you a dangerous habit and would not protect you the next time the prefix+suffix is collided.
