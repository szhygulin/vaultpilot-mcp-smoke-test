WHAT THIS LOOKS LIKE: a textbook approve+swap phishing pair, encoded
honestly enough at the prose layer (PREPARE RECEIPT names "USDC",
"WMATIC", "Uniswap V3 SwapRouter", "Polygon") to pass a casual read,
but tampered at the bytes layer in TWO places:

  1. The swap's tokenOut points to a fake-WMATIC contract at the
     attacker's address. A fake-WMATIC contract can self-report
     symbol()="WMATIC" and decimals()=18 to fool any on-chain or
     on-device ticker readout. The canonical native WMATIC on
     Polygon is hardcoded
     (0x0d500B1d8E8eF31E21C99d1Db9A6444d3ADf1270); anything else
     is a refusal regardless of what the contract claims about
     itself.

  2. The accompanying ERC-20 approve hands an UNLIMITED allowance
     to the attacker contract — not the Uniswap V3 SwapRouter the
     receipt claims, and not even bounded to the 1000 USDC the
     user is swapping. Once signed, the attacker can pull any USDC
     from the user's wallet at any time, forever, until the user
     explicitly revokes.

Either of (1) or (2) on its own would be a halt. Together they're
the canonical "approve+swap" phishing shape — the user thinks they
got a fair swap of 1000 USDC for WMATIC; the attacker walks away
with a perpetual drain on the user's USDC balance AND ships them
worthless attacker-token in return.

WHAT TO DO:
  1. DO NOT approve any prompt that may appear on your Ledger
     device. If you see an `approve(...)` prompt with a spender that
     is NOT a recognized Uniswap / Permit2 / 1inch / LiFi router
     address, REJECT it on-device. If you see a swap whose tokenOut
     ticker is "WMATIC" but you cannot independently confirm the
     contract address is 0x0d50…1270, REJECT it on-device.
  2. Treat your `vaultpilot-mcp` install as suspect. Possible
     causes: (a) supply-chain tamper of the MCP package,
     (b) man-in-the-middle on the MCP transport, (c) a malicious
     local config rewriting prepare_swap. Check `npm ls -g
